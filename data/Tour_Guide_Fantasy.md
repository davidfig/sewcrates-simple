<p class="normal">Author Harry Connolly's <a href="http://www.harryjconnolly.com/blog/?p=5488" title="explanation">explanation</a> of why he cancelled future <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Child-Fire-Twenty-Palaces-ebook/dp/B002PYFW9S/ref=tmm_kin_title_0?ie=UTF8&m=AG56TWVU5XWC2&qid=1342022924&sr=8-6" title="Twenty Palaces novels">Twenty Palaces novels</a>. I particularly loved his "tour guide" fantasy description:</p><p class="indent">Most urban fantasy is what I (lovingly!) refer to as “tour guide” fantasy: the protagonist is an expert in the setting and part of the fun is letting them lead the reader through the world. Even when the protagonist is a noob, an advisor character will show up right around the quarter story mark to explain the situation. It gives some context to the story and makes the stakes more concrete.</p><p class="indent">And that’s fine in most cases. But it’s annoying to see a supposed secret society giving Our Fresh-Faced Hero a guided tour of their headquarters, complete with an introduction to the irreplaceable person in charge. It’s not realistic and it doesn’t make sense.</p><p class="indent">So having the magic, players, dangers, and stakes inferred by the protagonist based on the events of the book–leaving things mysterious, in other words–was a choice, and many readers didn’t care for it.</p><p class="normal">The best part of the Twenty Palaces books for me was figuring out the mysteries of his magical system. It reminded me of discovering how magic worked in the <a href="http://www.amazon.com/The-Belgariad-Set-Books-1-5/dp/0345340442/ref=sr_1_4?ie=UTF8&qid=1342027858&sr=8-4&keywords=belgariad" title="Belgariad series">Belgariad series</a> by David & Leigh Eddings when I was a child. For lack of a better word, the discovery process itself was magical.</p>
