<p class="normal">The barbeque was to start promptly at noon. “Do you think anyone’s coming?” David asked Doolies.</p><p class="normal">Doolies sat next to him in the cabana reading a magazine. She wore three-quarter pants and held a magazine over her head to either block out the sun or read the article; David wasn’t sure which. “We invited eleven people, and we know my sister is coming, so, yes, someone will come.”</p><p class="normal">The cabana faced the small barbeque area, where they had piled the food and paper goods on the table. A purple dolphin balloon swam from a string above the table. David admired how the dolphin always kept its nose into the wind, a trait he first saw while piloting the shopping cart through the aisles.</p><p class="normal">David bit his lip and looked over the food again. He watched the dolphin twist in the wind for a bit. “Okay, I’ve done the math, I can eat six hamburgers and maybe four hot dogs, I figure you’re good for two of each, and your sister can down the same.”</p><p class="normal">“What are you talking about?” Doolies put down her magazine and turned on her chair. She wore a black hat with her ponytail sticking out. Her glasses, which darkened in the sun, looked black.</p><p class="normal">“I was figuring what we would do if nobody shows. We have to eat all this barbeque meat somehow, and I was divvying it up, is all. Are you trying to tell me you can’t eat two hot dogs and hamburgers?”</p><p class="normal">“First off, I’m the good eater of the relationship, so, yes, I can eat that much, you should worry more about your own eating. And second, they’ll show up, don’t worry so much. It’s not even noon yet.”</p><p class="normal">“You did tell them it starts at noon, right?”</p><p class="normal">“Well, sort of,” Doolies said. She flicked imaginary lint off her white shirt.</p><p class="normal">“What does that mean?”</p><p class="normal">“I told people to be here noonish.”</p>
